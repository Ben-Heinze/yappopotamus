#+TITLE: Earliest Deadline First (Client Scheduling)
#+OPTIONS: toc:nil num:nil

*Setup.* You are a plumber with a list of clients. Each client has a
deadline by which they need help, and a duration of work. A client is
angry for every hour by which their job runs past their deadline
(/lateness/). The goal is to schedule all jobs to minimize the maximum
lateness across clients.

* Ideas that fail

- *Earliest deadline first.* (Actually optimal — see below.)
- *Most schedule flexibility first.* Breaks when a smaller job has a lot
  of flexibility but an early deadline and a larger, less flexible job
  is preempted.
- *Shortest job first.* Breaks when a big job with a wide window is
  pushed behind small jobs and misses its deadline unnecessarily.

* Earliest Deadline First (EDF)

Sort jobs by deadline (earliest first) and schedule them back-to-back
starting at time $0$.

*Theorem.* EDF produces a schedule with optimal maximum lateness.

*Plan of attack.* Take any optimal schedule and modify it, preserving
optimality, until it matches EDF's schedule.

** Two ways EDF's schedule can differ from an arbitrary optimum

- *Ordering.* Multiple optimal schedules may exist with different job
  orderings.
- *Gaps.* An optimal schedule may leave idle gaps between jobs. EDF
  does not, but we can compare to schedules that do.

** Lemma: no-gap optimal schedule

*Lemma.* There exists an optimal schedule with no gap between jobs.

*Proof.* Given any optimal schedule with gaps, shift each job forward
into the gap immediately before it. This can only reduce a job's finish
time, so no job's lateness increases; the maximum lateness cannot go up.

** Lemma: no-inversion optimal schedule

*Definition.* A schedule has an *inversion* if some client is scheduled
before another client with an earlier deadline. EDF has no inversions.

*Lemma.* There exists an optimal schedule with no inversions.

*Proof sketch.*
- Any inversion is caused by some pair of /consecutive/ inverted
  clients (if all consecutive pairs are in order, there are no
  inversions).
- Swapping a consecutive inverted pair reduces the total number of
  inversions.
- Swapping does not increase the maximum lateness.

  The two swapped jobs occupy the same combined time slot before and
  after the swap. The client moved earlier finishes earlier (lateness
  decreases). The client moved later finishes later — but at worst its
  new finish time equals what the other client's finish time was in the
  original schedule, whose lateness we were already tolerating. All
  other clients are unaffected. So the maximum lateness cannot go up.

Repeatedly swapping consecutive inversions turns any optimal schedule
into an inversion-free optimal schedule — which, up to permutation of
equal-deadline jobs, is EDF's schedule.

*Does reordering equal-deadline jobs change the maximum lateness?* No.
The deadlines are the same and the total work is the same, so the
maximum lateness is unchanged.
