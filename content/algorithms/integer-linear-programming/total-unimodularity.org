#+TITLE: Total Unimodularity
#+OPTIONS: toc:nil num:nil

*Total Unimodularity.* A matrix is *totally unimodular* if the
determinant of every square submatrix is $0$, $1$, or $-1$.

Consequences:

- Totally unimodular matrices are made of entries in $\{-1, 0, 1\}$
  (since single entries are $1 \times 1$ submatrices).
- If $A$ is totally unimodular, so is $A^T$ (determinants are
  invariant under transpose).

* Why we care: Hoffman-Kruskal

*Theorem (Hoffman-Kruskal, 1956).*
$A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ is totally unimodular
$\iff$ for every integer vector $b \in \mathbb{Z}^m$, the feasible region
$\{x \in \mathbb{R}^n \mid A x \leq b,\; x \geq 0\}$ has integer
vertices.

Applied to an LP with objective $\max\; c^T x$ subject to
$A x \leq b$, $x \geq 0$: if $A$ is totally unimodular and $b$ is
integer, then the LP optimum sits at an integer vertex, so an LP
solver produces the ILP answer directly — in polynomial time.

* Testing for total unimodularity: Ghouila-Houri

For a long time there was no fast way to check total unimodularity
until Ghouila-Houri's theorem in 1962.

*Theorem (Ghouila-Houri, 1962).* $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ is
totally unimodular $\iff$ for every subset of rows $R$, there is a
partition $R = R_1 \cup R_2$ such that for every column $j$,
\[
\sum_{i \in R_1} A_{ij} - \sum_{i \in R_2} A_{ij} \in \{-1, 0, 1\}.
\]

The two theorems together give a workable recipe: prove the constraint
matrix satisfies Ghouila-Houri's condition, and Hoffman-Kruskal
guarantees the LP relaxation has integer optima.

* Two-sided constraints

We're only ever interested in constraints, not the objective. Recall
the bipartite matching ILP (see [[../approximation-algorithms/vertex-cover-approximation][vertex cover]]/matching), whose two
families of constraints are:

\[
\sum_{j \in N(i)} x_{ij} \leq 1 \quad \text{for all } i \in L
\]
\[
\sum_{i \in N(j)} x_{ij} \leq 1 \quad \text{for all } j \in R
\]

Stacked into matrix form, the /constraint matrix/ $A$ has a row per
node and a column per edge; entry $A_{ij}$ is $1$ if node $i$ is
incident to edge $j$. The right-hand side is $b = (1, 1, \ldots, 1)^T$,
one entry per node.

* Compact form and why it matters

The whole system is $A x \leq b$. Two conditions guarantee that the LP
relaxation produces an integer solution:

1. $A$ is totally unimodular.
2. $b$ is integer-valued.

For bipartite matching both hold: $A$ has the TU property, and $b$ is
all $1$s. The LP solver will never find it beneficial to return a
fractional $x_{ij}$.
