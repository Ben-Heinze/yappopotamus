* Unit III: Binomial Logistic Regression (Ch 20)

If \(W_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(\pi_i)\), then \(Y_i = \sum_{i=1}^{n} W_i \sim \text{Binomial}(m_i = n, \pi_i)\)

- \(Y_i\) is the total number of successes/yes out of \(m_i\) trials

#+begin_quote
*INFO Note:* \(\text{Bernoulli}(\pi_i)\) is equivalent to \(\text{Binomial}(m_i = 1, \pi_i)\)
#+end_quote

** Examples

- Measure \(m_i\) leaves on a plant and test each leaf for an infection, counting up number infected
- Test groups of \(m_i\) beetles together in containers at a concentration of insecticide, count number that die
- Visit one 10 km × 10 km grid cell and conduct 4 spatially "independent" visits by deploying ultrasonic microphones at those 4 locations. Collect acoustic data and record detection/non-detection data for a bat species of interest.

* Binomial GLM

** Differences from Binary Regression

Because each observation, \(Y_i\), is a bounded count \(\{0, 1, \ldots, m_i\}\) representing the sum of \(m_i\) identical Bernoulli trials, the probability of success/yes on each trial must be \(\pi_i\).

- There is a chance for *overdispersion* (extra binomial variation) if trials within each of our grouped binomial observations do not have the same probability of success.
- We will need to assess if this is the case!
- If the true probability varies within a set of \(m_i\) observations, they should not be combined!

* Can We Trust Our Results?

Residuals are helpful here!

1. *Deviance residual:* Sum of squared deviance residuals sums to the deviance statistic. Measure of discrepancy in the likelihood function due to the model fit at each observed count (See Sleuth for formula).

2. *Pearson residual:* Designed to have mean = 0 and sd = 1.

=plot(glm_fit)= plots Pearson resids.

*** Overdispersion Solutions

1. *Quasi-likelihood methods* adjust the variance with a multiplier relative to what would be generated by the binomial likelihood:

   - Regular variance assumed in binomial: \(\widehat{\text{Var}}\{Y_i \mid X\} = m_i \hat{\pi}_i (1 - \hat{\pi}_i)\)
   - Overdispersion corrected variance:
     \[\widehat{\text{Var}}[Y_i \mid X] = \hat{\psi}\, m_i \hat{\pi}_i (1 - \hat{\pi}_i)\]
     OR
     \[\widehat{\text{Var}}^*(S_i) = \hat{\psi}^*\, m_i \hat{\pi}_i (1 - \hat{\pi}_i)\]

** Two Estimates of \(\psi\)

The model assumes the dispersion parameter \(\psi\) is 1. An estimate \(\hat{\psi}\) = residual deviance / residual df:

- Pearson residuals: \(\hat{\psi}^* =\) [FILL IN] (used by =quasibinomial= family in =glm=)
- Deviance residuals: \(\hat{\psi} =\) [FILL IN]

Use the "fullish" model to estimate overdispersion!

*** Adjust "By Hand"

- Estimate the dispersion parameter: \(\hat{\psi} = \dfrac{\text{residual deviance}}{\text{residual df}}\)
- Multiply SEs using: \(SE^\dagger(\hat{\beta}_j) = SE(\hat{\beta}_j)\,\sqrt{\hat{\psi}}\)
- Use adjusted SEs in tests: \(t = \dfrac{\hat{\beta}_j}{SE^\dagger(\hat{\beta}_j)}\)
- Adjust drop-in-deviance tests as: \(F = \dfrac{\text{Drop in deviance} / d}{\hat{\psi}}\), where \(d\) is the difference in number of parameters between full and reduced; this should follow \(F(d,\; \text{residual df from full model})\)

Adjustments could also be similarly performed using \(\hat{\psi}^*\) and then match the =family = "quasibinomial"= results in =glm=.
